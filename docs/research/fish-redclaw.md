# Redclaw crayfish — aquaponics feasibility

> **Verdict: 🟠 challenging** · kind: fish · data id: `redclaw`
>
> Community-evidence research (forums, grower blogs, user accounts, commercial cases), 2026-06.

## Summary

Redclaw (Cherax quadricarinatus) is a hardy, low-aggression tropical crayfish that hobbyists routinely keep alive and breeding in aquaponics tanks, but the recurring practitioner and industry verdict is that tank/aquaponics culture is a poor MONEY-MAKER: every species-profile source (The Fish Site, Live Aquaponics/farmingcrawfish) states the only commercially viable redclaw operations are earthen-pond based because the animals depend on pond-bottom detritus and reach low individual yields in tanks. The biggest documented hurdle is juvenile cannibalism — early juveniles cannibalise heavily without abundant shelter, and tank-nursery survival drops sharply with density (62.7% at 66/m2 down to ~45% at 156/m2 in RAS trials). Grow-out reports converge on roughly 9-12 months to a ~65-80 g market animal, matching the model's 10-month default, but the model's 24-30C band, the absence of any FCR figure in field reports, and especially the €25/kg price all need scrutiny against German reality. In Germany the entire premium crayfish market is built on native Astacus astacus at ~€60/kg in starred restaurants; redclaw is bred there (Krebsgarten Mecklenburg, Nick Zimmer) in heated indoor RAS as a faster blue-shelled alternative, which proves it CAN be grown in a cold climate indoors, but real-world Berlin/Brandenburg demand and pricing for redclaw specifically is unproven.

## Pros

- Low aggression relative to other crayfish and 'cannibalism not regarded as an issue' in pond grow-out when shelter is ample (The Fish Site); hobbyists report easy survival and breeding in home aquaponics systems
- Warm-water + hiding-structure requirement maps cleanly onto a coupled aquaponics tank: stacked PVC pipes / mesh / blocks are the standard, cheap fix and growers confirm it works
- No migration/spawning-run needs; females self-spawn 100-1000 eggs and craylings emerge directly as miniatures, so on-site breeding is feasible (though Berlin model BUYS juveniles)
- Documented to grow to a sellable 65-80 g animal in roughly 9-12 months, consistent with the model's 10-month grow-out
- Plant side works: University of Wisconsin (Chris Groff) and a published aquaponics growth study both demonstrate peppers/produce can be grown on crayfish waste alone
- Already bred commercially indoors in cold Germany (Mecklenburg-Vorpommern) in heated RAS, so the climate barrier is solvable with heating

## Cons

- Strong, repeated industry consensus that TANK/recirculating redclaw culture is economically unviable — 'the only commercially viable operations for redclaw are earthen pond based'; tank production reportedly returns far less than operating, let alone setup, costs
- Juvenile cannibalism is the headline failure mode: 'juvenile redclaw become cannibalistic, leaving very few survivors'; RAS nursery survival fell to ~45% at higher densities
- Redclaw relies on pond-bottom detritus for good growth, which a clean coupled aquaponics tank does not provide — growth in tanks is slower than the pond-based 9-month figure
- Crayfish need much more floor AREA than fish per kg (pond stocking only 5-15/m2; broodstock 1-2/m2), so kg-yield-per-tank-footprint is low, undercutting €/m2 economics
- Escape behaviour: a US aquaponics blogger gave up after crayfish repeatedly climbed out of the system; tanks must be fully escape-proofed
- No field source reports an FCR anywhere near a single clean number — the 1.6 biological FCR in the model is not corroborated by any practitioner account
- Germany's premium crayfish demand is anchored on native Astacus astacus (~€60/kg); redclaw is a niche/novelty product there with unproven retail pricing and possible consumer-familiarity gap

- **[commercial-case]** lebensmittelmagazin.de — 'Im Krebsgang', Edelkrebse aus Mecklenburg-Vorpommern (https://www.lebensmittelmagazin.de/wirtschaft/20200724-...)

- **[commercial-case]** garcon24.de — 'Blauer Flusskrebs aus sächsischer Aquakultur' / Nick Zimmer (https://garcon24.de/2018/04/20/blauer-flusskrebs-aus-saechsischer-aquakultur/)

## Model check (vs `src/data` defaults)

Grow-out / cycle time: GOOD match — field reports cluster at 9-12 months to a 65-80 g animal (Fish Site 9 mo/65g; German breeders 12 mo/70-80g), so the model's 10-month default is realistic, BUT note the 9-month figure is pond-based and tanks grow slower, so 10 months in a coupled tank may undershoot harvest weight. Temperature: GOOD — sources give optimum ~23-31C / 24-29C, so the model's 24-30C band is right (reproduction needs >23C; mortality below ~13C — meaning Berlin winters force year-round heating cost the model should capture). FCR 1.6: UNSUPPORTED — no practitioner or industry source reports a clean FCR; redclaw are detritivores/omnivores partly feeding on tank biofilm and waste, so a fish-style 1.6 is speculative and possibly optimistic for a clean tank. Price €25/kg: QUESTIONABLE direction-wise — global farm-gate is only ~US$12.50/kg (~€11-12) and wholesale tops out ~US$18/kg for >120g animals, so €25/kg is well ABOVE commodity pricing; it is only plausible if sold as a live premium novelty in Germany, where the comparable native Astacus fetches ~€60/kg — so €25/kg is defensible as a niche direct-sale price but not as a commodity price. Difficulty 'Medium': arguably UNDERSTATED for a tank/coupled system — the dominant industry message is that tank redclaw is economically marginal due to detritus dependence, low area-yield and juvenile cannibalism; 'Medium-Hard' is more honest. Juvenile cost €3/kg produced: not directly corroborated by sources (Berlin model buys juveniles), and craylings/juveniles are small/cheap to produce but survival losses in tank nursery (~45-63%) inflate effective per-kg juvenile cost.

## Germany / EU notes

Real German activity exists but is small and niche. (1) Sourcing: redclaw juveniles/broodstock are available within the EU and bred domestically — e.g. Nick Zimmer's Saxon operation rears blue Cherax quadricarinatus indoors (garcon24.de) and a Mecklenburg 'Krebsgarten' runs indoor RAS; specialist shops (edelkrebse-shop.de) and the German aquaponik-forum.de / selbstvers.org communities discuss crayfish-in-aquaponics, though most German hobbyists pursue native Astacus astacus, not redclaw. (2) Regulation: redclaw is a non-native tropical species and CANNOT survive German winters outdoors (mortality below ~13C), so it is effectively contained to heated indoor systems — this aligns with EU Invasive Alien Species concerns; Cherax quadricarinatus is widely farmed but growers should verify it is not restricted under EU/German IAS rules and ensure escape-proofing (federal/Länder aquaculture and animal-keeping permits apply). (3) Winter climate fit: poor without heating — a Berlin/Brandenburg coupled system needs year-round 24-30C, a real and continuous energy cost; the upside is an indoor RAS sidesteps the crayfish-plague and mink predation that plague outdoor German crayfish farming. (4) Market demand: Germany has an established PREMIUM live-crayfish market, but it is anchored on native noble crayfish at ~€60/kg into upscale/starred restaurants; redclaw sells there only as a blue-shelled novelty/'freshwater lobster' to specialty restaurants and Asian markets, so the €25/kg model price is plausible as a niche live-sale figure but demand is thin and must be cultivated — there is no evidence of established commercial redclaw aquaponics in Germany, only indoor RAS breeders and hobby attempts.
